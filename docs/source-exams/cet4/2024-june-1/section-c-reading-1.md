# CET-4 2024-june-1 Section C Reading 1

## Passage
Lao Zi once said, “Care about what other people think and you will always be their prisoner.”

People-pleasing, or seeking self-worth through others’ approval, is unproductive and an exhausting way to go through life. Why do we allow what others think of us to have so much power over how we feel about ourselves? If it’s true that you can’t please all people all of the time, wouldn’t it make sense to stop trying?

Unfortunately, sense often isn’t driving our behavior. For social beings who desire love and belonging, wanting to be liked, and caring about the effect we have on others, is healthy and allows us to make connections.

However, where we get into trouble is when our self-worth is dependent upon whether we win someone’s approval or not.

This need to be liked can be traced back to when we were children and were completely dependent on others to take care of us: Small children are not just learning how to walk and communicate, they are also trying to learn how the world works. We learn about who we are and what is expected of us based on interactions with others, so, to a four-year-old, if Mommy or Daddy doesn’t like him or her, there is the danger that they will abandon them. We need to understand that when we desperately want someone to approve of us, it’s being driven by that little kid part of us that is still terrified of abandonment.

As you become more capable of providing yourself with the approval you seek, your need for external validation will start to vanish, leaving you stronger, more confident, and yes, happier in your life. Imagine how much time we lose each moment we restrain our authentic selves in an effort to be liked.

If we base our worth on the opinions of others, we cheat ourselves of the power to shape our experiences and embrace life not only for others but also for ourselves, because ultimately, there is no difference. So embrace the cliché (老话) and love yourself as it’s highly doubtful that you’ll regret it.

## Questions

### 46
Prompt: What can we conclude from Lao Zi’s quotation?
- A. We should see through other people’s attempt to make a prisoner of us.
- B. We can never really please other people even if we try as hard as we can.
- C. We can never be truly free if taking to heart others’ opinion of us.
- D. We should care about other people’s view as much as they care about our own.

Answer: C
Explanation: Lao Zi’s quote “Care about what other people think and you will always be their prisoner” directly conveys that if we care too much about others’ opinions, we will lose our freedom. Option C aligns with this meaning, as “taking to heart others’ opinion of us” corresponds to “care about what other people think” and “never be truly free” corresponds to “always be their prisoner.” Option A misinterprets the quote as others attempting to imprison us, which is not the case. Option B is about the impossibility of pleasing everyone, which is a later point in the passage, not the direct conclusion from Lao Zi’s quote. Option D is the opposite of the quote’s message.

### 47
Prompt: What will happen if we base our self-worth on other people’s approval?
- A. Our desire to be loved will be fulfilled.
- B. Our life will be unfruitful and exhausting.
- C. Our identity as social beings will be affected.
- D. Our sense of self will be sharpened and enhanced.

Answer: B
Explanation: The passage states that “People-pleasing, or seeking self-worth through others’ approval, is unproductive and an exhausting way to go through life.” “Unproductive” matches “unfruitful” in option B, and “exhausting” is directly used. Option A is incorrect because the passage does not state that this behavior fulfills the desire to be loved; in fact, it is an unhealthy dependency. Option C is not mentioned; the passage says caring about others’ effect is healthy for social beings, not that basing self-worth on approval affects identity. Option D is the opposite of the passage’s message, as basing self-worth on others weakens one’s sense of self.

### 48
Prompt: What may account for our need to be liked or approved of?
- A. Our desperate longing for interactions with others.
- B. Our understanding of the workings of the world.
- C. Our knowledge about the pain of abandonment.
- D. Our early childhood fear of being deserted.

Answer: D
Explanation: The passage traces the need to be liked back to childhood: “This need to be liked can be traced back to when we were children... it’s being driven by that little kid part of us that is still terrified of abandonment.” “Terrified of abandonment” matches “fear of being deserted” in option D. Option A is not the root cause; interactions with others are how we learn, not the reason for the need to be liked. Option B is a result of childhood learning, not the cause. Option C is incorrect because it is a fear of abandonment, not knowledge of the pain, that drives the need.

### 49
Prompt: What can we do when we become better able to provide ourselves with the desired approval?
- A. Enjoy a happier life.
- B. Exercise self-restraint.
- C. Receive more external validation.
- D. Strengthen our power of imagination.

Answer: A
Explanation: The passage says, “As you become more capable of providing yourself with the approval you seek, your need for external validation will start to vanish, leaving you stronger, more confident, and yes, happier in your life.” “Happier in your life” directly matches option A. Option B is incorrect because self-restraint is what we lose when we stop seeking others’ approval. Option C is the opposite; the need for external validation vanishes. Option D is not mentioned; imagination is used as an example to highlight wasted time, not a result of self-approval.

### 50
Prompt: What does the author advise us to do in the last paragraph?
- A. Embrace life for ourselves and for others.
- B. Base our worth on others’ opinions.
- C. See our experiences as assets.
- D. Love ourselves as we are.

Answer: D
Explanation: The last paragraph concludes with “So embrace the cliché (老话) and love yourself as it’s highly doubtful that you’ll regret it.” “Love yourself” matches option D. Option A is a partial statement; the passage says embracing life for ourselves and others is a result of not basing worth on others, but the direct advice is to love ourselves. Option B is the opposite of the author’s advice. Option C is not mentioned; the passage talks about shaping our experiences, not seeing them as assets.
