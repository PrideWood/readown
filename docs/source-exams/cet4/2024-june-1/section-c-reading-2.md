# CET-4 2024-june-1 Section C Reading 2

## Passage
Some people have said aging is more a slide into forgetfulness than a journey towards wisdom. However, a growing body of research suggests that late-in-life learning is possible. In reality, education does an aging brain good.

Throughout life, people’s brains constantly renovate themselves. In the late 1960s, British brain scientist Geoffrey Raisman spied growth in damaged brain regions of rats through an electron microscope; their brains were forging new connections. This meant brains may change every time a person learns something new.

Of course, that doesn’t mean the brain isn’t affected by the effects of time. Just as height usually declines over the years, so does brain volume: Humans lose about 4 percent every decade starting in their 40s. But that reduction doesn’t necessarily make people think slower; as long as we are alive and functioning, we can alter our brains with new information and experiences.

In fact, scientists now suspect accumulating novel experiences, facts, and skills can keep people’s minds more flexible. New pathways can strengthen our ever-changing mental structure, even as the brain shrinks.

Conventional fixes like word puzzles and brain-training apps can contribute to mental durability. Even something as simple as taking a different route to the grocery store or going somewhere new on vacation can keep the brain healthy.

A desire for new life challenges can further boost brainpower. Research about aging adults who take on new enterprises shows improved function and memory as well as a reduced risk of mental disease. Openness—a characteristic defined by curiosity and a desire for knowledge—may also help pass brain tests. Some folks are born with this take-in-the-world attitude, but those who aren’t as genetically gifted aren’t necessarily out of luck. While genes can encourage an interest in doing new things, a 2012 study in the journal Psychology and Aging found completing reasoning tasks like puzzles and number games can enhance that desire for novel experiences, which can, in turn, refresh the brain. That’s why brain scientist Richard Kennedy says “It’s not that old dogs can’t learn new tricks. It’s that maybe old dogs don’t realize why they should.”

## Questions

### 51
Prompt: What do some people think of aging adults?
- A) Their wisdom grows as time goes by.
- B) Their memory gradually deteriorates.
- C) They can benefit from late-in-life learning.
- D) They are likely to have mental health issues.

Answer: B
Explanation: The first sentence states, “Some people have said aging is more a slide into forgetfulness than a journey towards wisdom.” “Slide into forgetfulness” directly corresponds to “memory gradually deteriorates” in option B. Option A is the opposite of what some people think. Option C is the author’s view, not the view of “some people.” Option D is not mentioned in the passage.

### 52
Prompt: What can we conclude from Geoffrey Raisman’s finding?
- A) Brain damage seriously hinders one’s learning.
- B) Brain power weakens slower than we imagine.
- C) Brains can refresh and improve with learning.
- D) Brains forge connections under new conditions.

Answer: C
Explanation: Geoffrey Raisman observed that damaged rat brain regions were forging new connections, and the passage concludes, “This meant brains may change every time a person learns something new.” This shows that learning leads to brain changes, which aligns with “brains can refresh and improve with learning” in option C. Option A is incorrect because the finding shows growth in damaged regions, not hindrance. Option B is not related to Raisman’s finding. Option D is too narrow; the finding is about learning, not just “new conditions.”

### 53
Prompt: What is one thing that helps maintain the health of our brain even as it shrinks?
- A) Doing daily routines by conventional means.
- B) Avoiding worrying about our mental durability.
- C) Imitating old dogs’ way of learning new tricks.
- D) Approaching everyday tasks in novel ways.

Answer: D
Explanation: The passage states, “Even something as simple as taking a different route to the grocery store or going somewhere new on vacation can keep the brain healthy,” and “accumulating novel experiences... can keep people’s minds more flexible.” “Taking a different route” and “going somewhere new” are examples of “approaching everyday tasks in novel ways,” which matches option D. Option A is the opposite; conventional routines do not help. Option B is not mentioned. Option C is a metaphor used at the end, not a practical method.

### 54
Prompt: What does the author say can contribute to the improvement of brain function?
- A) Being curious and desiring knowledge.
- B) Being eager to pass brain tests at an old age.
- C) Rising to life’s challenges and avoiding risks.
- D) Boosting immunity to serious mental diseases.

Answer: A
Explanation: The passage mentions, “Openness—a characteristic defined by curiosity and a desire for knowledge—may also help pass brain tests,” and “a desire for new life challenges can further boost brainpower.” “Curiosity and a desire for knowledge” directly matches option A. Option B misinterprets “pass brain tests” as a goal, not a result. Option C is incorrect because the passage encourages taking on new challenges, not avoiding risks. Option D is a result, not a cause, of improved brain function.

### 55
Prompt: What is the finding of the 2012 study in the journal Psychology and Aging?
- A) Wishing to solve puzzles enhances one’s reasoning power.
- B) Playing number games unexpectedly stimulates one’s memory.
- C) Desiring new experiences can help to renovate the brain.
- D) Learning new tricks should not be confined to old dogs only.

Answer: C
Explanation: The 2012 study found that “completing reasoning tasks like puzzles and number games can enhance that desire for novel experiences, which can, in turn, refresh the brain.” “Refresh the brain” corresponds to “renovate the brain,” and the core finding is that the desire for new experiences leads to brain renewal, matching option C. Option A reverses the logic; the study says puzzles enhance the desire for new experiences, not that wishing to solve puzzles enhances reasoning. Option B is not the focus of the study. Option D is a quote from Richard Kennedy, not the study’s finding.
